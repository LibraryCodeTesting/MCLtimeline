  • 1923 - March 29

    With strong support from the County Board of Agriculture, the Grange, and the County Agent, the Monmouth County Library system is established by the County Board of Freeholders after a majority of residents vote in favor of starting a county library.

    To help create the library system, the Monmouth County Library Commission appoints Elinor E. Randall as the first County Librarian. Randall’s job begins on May 1st.

    It is also reported that that the library is expected to be fully functioning on September 1st.

  • 1923 - June 11

    Station libraries are now being established with the goal of making books more easily available to local residents than if they had a city library system. The stations are a mix of local places - public or private - which will house circulating collections for the public to use.

    While the County Library Commission explores the area to find locations for library stations, the first stations are established at Holmdel, Rumson, and at the Woman’s Club in Ocean Grove.

    Every other month, each station will be visited by the county's book automobile and will receive different books to circulate. The library's automobile can hold 800 books, and also has space for boxes of phonograph records and pictures. It was made from a refurbished truck and weighs more than 1 ton.

    Outside of book stations, residents can also borrow items by writing to the Librarian’s Office at the Courthouse in Freehold.

  • 1924 - December 4

    Bertram Borden, Chairman of the Housing Committee for the Monmouth County Library, requests that the county review options for making additional space for the library's headquarters.

    Just one month after fully opening, Bertram says the library has, “completely outgrown its quarters in the basement of the courthouse.”

  • 1924 - December 4

    There are now 23,875 books in Monmouth County Library's book collection, and they are accessible to the public at 40 library stations.

    A professional relationship has also been established with local schools. Monmouth County Library now provides additional children’s books directly to schools, as well as suppletory reference materials for teachers to use.

    The partnership with schools began after a survey was sent to every school in the county. 41 of the county's schools answered, and the Library Commission visited 26 schools in-person. Out of the 26 schools that were visited, 11 are selected to house circulating children’s collections.

  • 1925 - July 6

    It is reported that the library’s book automobile has delivered 35,183 books over 515 deliveries to library stations.

    In one month alone, 2,000 books were distributed to the public via library stations , of which 2,331 circulate through use of a car, and 332 books circulate from in-person visits, and 169 are then mailed out to recipients.

  • 1926 - February 8

    Monmouth County Library's Headquarters Branch in Freehold has now been expanded into a remodeled boiler room behind the courthouse

    At this time, the library is supported by a tax of 0.2 mill, which comes from the towns who voted to join into the county library system. The tax provided $20,770 to the library for work functions, and another $1,280 is also provided by the state, to help the library serve local schools.

    The county library system now serves 50 schools regularly. Students and teachers can request items for recreational use or classroom instruction. The items are then delivered to to schools via the book automobile.

    There are now 116 distribution stations, and the book automobile visits each one every 2 months.

    The largest book stations are housed at Allentown, Atlantic Highlands, Brielle, Farmingdale, Keansburg, Ocean Grove, and Red Bank.

    Library stations that housed over 500 books could be found in Adelphia, Allenhurst, Clarksburg, Deal, Holmel, Interlaken, Jerseyville, Millhurst, Perrineville, Seabright, Shrewbury, Wayside, West Belmar, and West Farms.

    Library stations that housed over 400 books could be found in Ardens, Colt’s Neck, Eatontown, Highlands, Marlboro, Monmouth Beach, Oakhurst, West Long Branch

    The library states that its goal for 1927 is to increase demand for the library stations.

    There are now 36,557 books in the collection, and 3,738 of the new books resulted directly from patron requests.

    County librarians say that patrons often request for materials that help improve work skills among manual laborers – such as metal workers, plumbers, and carpenters.

    The library system also has 11,170 registered patrons now, and borrowers report that they do not only borrow items for themselves, but also borrow items for their family members and neighbors as well.

    Books can be borrowed as long as needed to finish them, and the time can be extended when they are used for studies, or if someone lives far from a distribution center.

  • 1927 - January 24

    The library continues to grow quickly!

    The book collection has circulated 41,268 times in the last year – which was an increase of 10,000 more books compared to the year before that.

    To meet the increased demand, the number of library staff also grows, and some locations now shift from volunteer-run staffing to paid staffing – such as at the Keansburg location.

    The books housed at each book station are tailored to fit each community’s preferences and needs – which are relayed to the county library by those overseeing each station.

    The Headquarters Branch in Freehold is also sreporting that the current location is becoming too crowded again.

  • 1927 - October 19

    Monmouth County Library reports that it is now distributing around 4,000 books in 70 different schools.

  • 1928 - January 12

    The county library report shows that a total of 51,220 books were circulated in 1971, and 37,243 of the books were checked out via library stations.

  • 1928 - April

    There are now 135 library stations: 12 in stores, 24 in homes – mostly in farmers’ homes, 5 in library buildings, 3 in post offices, 8 in borough halls, 1 in a chapel, 1 at a road station, 1 in a parish house, 1 in a women’s clubhouse, 2 in a Young Men’s Christian Association building, 5 in grange halls, 2 in community centers, 2 in churches, 1 in a summer camp, 1 in a firehouse, 2 in high school libraries, 18 in schools that circulate to adults as well as children, and 46 in schools that only serve children. Each station houses between 50 to 1,000 books.

    Schools are now using 5,517 of Monmouth County Library's reference books, and the book automobile currently has 25 routes that must each be served once every two months. Each route has between two to six stations.

    The county library also reports using the following tactics to acquaint people with the system’s services:

    The librarian joined the grange to have direct contact with farmers and learn their needs.

    The Librarian gave lectures at meetings with the Grange, the Young Men’s Christian Association, Women’s Clubs, at schools, and other organizations.

    Newspapers give articles on library access and new or specialized books.

    The book automobile was made with distinct marketing to make it easily recognizable.

    Library exhibits are made especially for the 5-day county farm bureau meeting, which includes books on agriculture, books for farm women and farm children, map showing library station locations, useful charts, and more.

    “Effective use is made of the library camera.”

    Cooperation is maintained with other agencies, such as the County Health Office, Parent-Teacher Associations, the Boy Scouts of America, the Young Men’s Christian Association, the County Board of Agriculture, the County Social Service Organization, School Boards, Women’s Clubs, Mission-Study Classes, the Grange, the Farm Bureau, Churches, and Schools.

    Examples of helping:

    A farmer requested a trade book that wasn’t in the library. The library requested it from 6 other libraries, the State Library, and the Publisher without success. Then, the Secretary of the State Library Commission borrowed a private copy for the farmer.

    A farm boy with little chance for schooling borrowed a book on stationary engineering. After studying it all winter, they passed an exam to become a stationary engineer and secured a good position of employment.

    A sheep herder borrowed a book on animal diseases which his sheep became sick and he saved his herd – while other flocks perishes.

    When the local potato crop failed and diversification became necessary, the library was ready with truck-garden, fruit, dairy, and poultry books.
