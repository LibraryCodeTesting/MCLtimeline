  • 1923 - March 29

    With strong support from the County Board of Agriculture, the Grange, and the County Agent, the Monmouth County Library system was established by the County Board of Freeholders after the majority of residents vote in favor of the idea.
    Eleven towns and one township do not vote to join the county library, since they have local libraries of their own that they pay taxes towards already. Five towns elect to pay taxes to both a local town library and the county library.
    The Monmouth County Library Commission appoints Elinor E. Randall as the first county librarian. Randall will make plans for the development of the entire library system starting on May 1st, and the library will be functioning fully on September 1st.

  • 1923 - June 11

    Station libraries - housed within private locations such as local homes - are established at Holmdel, Rumson, and at the Woman’s Club in Ocean Grove. The county library commission is also exploring the area further to find more locations of interest.
    The goal of the stations is to make books more easily available to people than if they had a city library system.
    Outside of book stations, residents can also request titles by writing to the Librarian’s Office at the Courthouse in Freehold, and the book trailer will drive to each library station once every 2 months to change each station’s book collections.
    The book trailer can hold 800 books and has space for boxes of phonograph records and pictures. It was made from a refurbished truck that weighs more than 1 ton.

  • 1924 - December 4

    Mrs. Bertram Borden, Chairman of the Housing Committee for the Monmouth County Library, requests that the county review options for making additional space for the library, saying that the library has “completely outgrown its quarters in the basement of the court house”.

  • 1924 - December 4

    Mrs. Bertram Borden, Chairman of the Housing Committee for the Monmouth County Library, requests that the county review options for making additional space for the library, saying that the library has “completely outgrown its quarters in the basement of the court house”.

  • 1924 - December 4

    The county system’s book collection expands to 23,875 books that are available at 40 library stations.
    A professional relationship has also been established with local schools to provide additional children’s books and suppletory reference materials for the schools’ curriculums.
    The partnership began after a survey was sent to every school in the county. 41 schools answered the survey, and the Library Commission also visited 26 schools in-person. Out of the 26 visited, 11 are selected to house circulating children’s collections.

  • 1925 - July 6

    Circulation statistics snapshot:
    The library’s book trailer reports making 515 deliveries to library stations, delivering 35,183 books over time.
    In one month, 2,000 books are distributed to the library stations, of which 2,331 circulate through use of a car, and 332 books circulate from in-person visits, and 169 are then mailed out to recipients.

  • 1926 - February 8

    In 1926, a library tax of 0.2 mill provides $20,770 to help maintain the library, while another $1,280 is provided by the state to help serve schools.
    There are now 116 distribution stations that the book trailer each visits every 2 months.
    11,170 borrowers are registered with the library, and registrants report sharing their borrowing privileges within families and among neighbors.
    Books can be borrowed as long as needed to finish them, and the time can be extended when they are used for studies, or if someone lives far from a distribution center.
    The collection now has 36,557 books to give out, fulfilled 3,738 books requests, and has added 6,501 more books over the previous year. Librarians report often assisting patrons in finding materials that help improve their job skills – such as metal workers, plumbers, and carpenters.
    The library also serves 50 schools, where students and teachers can also request library materials for recreational reading or for use in classroom instruction. The material is then delivered directly by the book trailer.
    The largest stations include Allentown, Atlantic Highlands, Brielle, Farmingdale, Keansburg, Ocean Grove, and Red Bank.
    The following are considered medium-sized stations that house over 500 books – Adelphia, Allenhurst, Clarksburg, Deal, Holmel, Interlaken, Jerseyville, Millhurst, Perrineville, Seabright, Shrewbury, Wayside, West Belmar, and West Farms.
    The following are considered medium-sized stations that house over 400 books – Ardens, Colt’s Neck, Eatontown, Highlands, Marlboro, Monmouth Beach, Oakhurst, West Long Branch
    Headquarters Branch in Freehold has been expanded into a remodeled boiler room behind the courthouse, and the goal for 1927 is to increase demand for the library stations.

  • 1927 - January 24

    The library continues to grow quickly. The book collection reportedly circulates 41,268 times – an increase of 10,000 more books over the prior year.
    Books housed at different book stations are tailored to fit each community’s preferences and needs – which are relayed to the library by those working each station.
    To meet the increased demand, the number of library staff also grows, and some locations now shift from volunteer-run staffing to paid staffing – such as at the Keansburg location.
    Headquarters Branch in Freehold is also saying the new building is becoming too crowded again.

  • 1927 - October 19

    Monmouth County Library reports distributing about 4,000 books in 70 schools.

  • 1928 - January 12

    Circulation statistics snapshot:
    A total of 51,220 books have been loaned. 37,243 of the books are checked out via library stations.

  • 1928 - April

    There are now 135 library stations: 12 in stores, 24 in homes – mostly in farmers’ homes, 5 in library buildings, 3 in post offices, 8 in borough halls, 1 in a chapel, 1 at a road station, 1 in a parish house, 1 in a women’s clubhouse, 2 in a Young Men’s Christian Association building, 5 in grange halls, 2 in community centers, 2 in churches, 1 in a summer camp, 1 in a firehouse, 2 in high school libraries, 18 in schools that circulate to adults as well as children, and 46 in schools that only serve children. Each station houses between 50 to 1,000 books.
    The library reports having 9,847 registered borrowers that circulated books 411,268 books – which is an increase of 10,000 circulation from the year before.
    Schools are using 5,517 reference books.
    The book trailer now has 25 routes that must each be served once every two months, and each route has between two to six stations.
    The County Library also reports using the following tactics to acquaint people with the system’s services:
    The librarian joined the grange to have direct contact with farmers and learn their needs.
    The Librarian gave lectures at meetings with the Grange, the Young Men’s Christian Association, Women’s Clubs, at schools, and other organizations.
    Newspapers give articles on library access and new or specialized books.
    The book automobile was made with distinct marketing to make it easily recognizable.
    Library exhibits are made especially for the 5-day county farm bureau meeting, which includes books on agriculture, books for farm women and farm children, map showing library station locations, useful charts, and more.
    “Effective use is made of the library camera.”
    Cooperation is maintained with other agencies, such as the County Health Office, Parent-Teacher Associations, the Boy Scouts of America, the Young Men’s Christian Association, the County Board of Agriculture, the County Social Service Organization, School Boards, Women’s Clubs, Mission-Study Classes, the Grange, the Farm Bureau, Churches, and Schools.
    Examples of helping:
    A farmer requested a trade book that wasn’t in the library. The library requested it from 6 other libraries, the State Library, and the Publisher without success. Then, the Secretary of the State Library Commission borrowed a private copy for the farmer.
    A farm boy with little chance for schooling borrowed a book on stationary engineering. After studying it all winter, they passed an exam to become a stationary engineer and secured a good position of employment.
    A sheep herder borrowed a book on animal diseases which his sheep became sick and he saved his herd – while other flocks perishes.
    When the local potato crop failed and diversification became necessary, the library was ready with truck-garden, fruit, dairy, and poultry books.
