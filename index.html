  • 1923 - March 29

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    With strong support from the County Board of Agriculture, the Grange, and the County Agent, the Monmouth County Library system is established by the County Board of Freeholders after a majority of residents vote in favor of starting the organization.

    To help create the library system, the Monmouth County Library Commission appoints Elinor E. Randall as the first County Librarian. Randall’s job begins on May 1st.

    It is also reported that that the library is expected to be fully functioning on September 1st.

  • 1923 - June 11

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Station libraries are now being established with the goal of making books more easily available to local residents than if they had a city library system. The stations are a mix of local places - public or private - which will house circulating collections for the public to use.

    While the County Library Commission explores the area to find locations for library stations, the first stations are established at Holmdel, Rumson, and at the Woman’s Club in Ocean Grove.

    Every other month, each station will be visited by the county's book automobile and will receive different books to circulate. The library's automobile can hold 800 books, and also has space for boxes of phonograph records and pictures. It was made from a refurbished truck and weighs more than 1 ton.

    Outside of book stations, residents can also borrow items by writing to the Librarian’s Office at the Courthouse in Freehold.

  • 1924 - December 4

    Bertram Borden, Chairman of the Housing Committee for the Monmouth County Library, requests that the county review options for making additional space for the library's headquarters.

    Just one month after fully opening, Bertram says the library has, “completely outgrown its quarters in the basement of the courthouse.”

  • 1924 - December 4

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    There are now 23,875 books in Monmouth County Library's book collection, and they are accessible to the public at 40 library stations.

    A professional relationship has also been established with local schools. Monmouth County Library now provides additional children’s books directly to schools, as well as suppletory reference materials for teachers to use.

    The partnership with schools began after a survey was sent to every school in the county. 41 of the county's schools answered, and the Library Commission visited 26 schools in-person. Out of the 26 schools that were visited, 11 are selected to house circulating children’s collections.

  • 1925 - July 6

    It is reported that the library’s book automobile has delivered 35,183 books over 515 deliveries to library stations.

    In one month alone, 2,000 books were distributed to the public via library stations , of which 2,331 circulate through use of a car, and 332 books circulate from in-person visits, and 169 are then mailed out to recipients.

  • 1926 - February 8

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County Library's Headquarters Branch in Freehold has now been expanded into a remodeled boiler room behind the courthouse

    At this time, the library is supported by a tax of 0.2 mill, which comes from the towns who voted to join into the county library system. The tax provided $20,770 to the library for work functions, and another $1,280 is also provided by the state, to help the library serve local schools.

    The county library system now serves 50 schools regularly. Students and teachers can request items for recreational use or classroom instruction. The items are then delivered to to schools via the book automobile.

    There are now 116 distribution stations, and the book automobile visits each one every 2 months.

    The largest book stations are housed at Allentown, Atlantic Highlands, Brielle, Farmingdale, Keansburg, Ocean Grove, and Red Bank.

    Library stations that housed over 500 books could be found in Adelphia, Allenhurst, Clarksburg, Deal, Holmel, Interlaken, Jerseyville, Millhurst, Perrineville, Seabright, Shrewbury, Wayside, West Belmar, and West Farms.

    Library stations that housed over 400 books could be found in Ardens, Colt’s Neck, Eatontown, Highlands, Marlboro, Monmouth Beach, Oakhurst, West Long Branch

    The library states that its goal for 1927 is to increase demand for the library stations.

    There are now 36,557 books in the collection, and 3,738 of the new books resulted directly from patron requests.

    County librarians say that patrons often request for materials that help improve work skills among manual laborers – such as metal workers, plumbers, and carpenters.

    The library system also has 11,170 registered patrons now, and borrowers report that they do not only borrow items for themselves, but also borrow items for their family members and neighbors as well.

    Books can be borrowed as long as needed to finish them, and the time can be extended when they are used for studies, or if someone lives far from a distribution center.

  • 1927 - January 24

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    The library continues to grow quickly!

    The book collection has circulated 41,268 times in the last year – which was an increase of 10,000 more books compared to the year before that.

    To meet the increased demand, the number of library staff also grows, and some locations now shift from volunteer-run staffing to paid staffing – such as at the Keansburg location.

    The books housed at each book station are tailored to fit each community’s preferences and needs – which are relayed to the county library by those overseeing each station.

    The Headquarters Branch in Freehold is also sreporting that the current location is becoming too crowded again.

  • 1927 - October 19

    Monmouth County Library reports that it is now distributing around 4,000 books in 70 different schools.

  • 1928 - January 12

    The county library report shows that a total of 51,220 books were circulated in 1971, and 37,243 of the books were checked out via library stations.

  • 1928 - April

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    There are now 135 library stations: 12 in stores, 24 in homes – mostly in farmers’ homes, 5 in library buildings, 3 in post offices, 8 in borough halls, 1 in a chapel, 1 at a road station, 1 in a parish house, 1 in a women’s clubhouse, 2 in a Young Men’s Christian Association building, 5 in grange halls, 2 in community centers, 2 in churches, 1 in a summer camp, 1 in a firehouse, 2 in high school libraries, 18 in schools that circulate to adults as well as children, and 46 in schools that only serve children. Each station houses between 50 to 1,000 books.

    Schools are now using 5,517 of Monmouth County Library's reference books, and the book automobile currently has 25 routes that must each be served once every two months. Each route has between two to six stations.

    The county library also reports using the following tactics to acquaint people with the system’s services:

    The librarian joined the grange to have direct contact with farmers and learn their needs.

    The Librarian gave lectures at meetings with the Grange, the Young Men’s Christian Association, Women’s Clubs, at schools, and other organizations.

    Newspapers give articles on library access and new or specialized books.

    The book automobile was made with distinct marketing to make it easily recognizable.

    Library exhibits are made especially for the 5-day county farm bureau meeting, which includes books on agriculture, books for farm women and farm children, map showing library station locations, useful charts, and more.

    “Effective use is made of the library camera.”

    Cooperation is maintained with other agencies, such as the County Health Office, Parent-Teacher Associations, the Boy Scouts of America, the Young Men’s Christian Association, the County Board of Agriculture, the County Social Service Organization, School Boards, Women’s Clubs, Mission-Study Classes, the Grange, the Farm Bureau, Churches, and Schools.

    Examples of helping:

    A farmer requested a trade book that wasn’t in the library. The library requested it from 6 other libraries, the State Library, and the Publisher without success. Then, the Secretary of the State Library Commission borrowed a private copy for the farmer.

    A farm boy with little chance for schooling borrowed a book on stationary engineering. After studying it all winter, they passed an exam to become a stationary engineer and secured a good position of employment.

    A sheep herder borrowed a book on animal diseases which his sheep became sick and he saved his herd – while other flocks perishes.

    When the local potato crop failed and diversification became necessary, the library was ready with truck-garden, fruit, dairy, and poultry books.

  • 1931 - November 12

    It is announced in the local paper that Monmouth County Library will march in the Armistice Day with a float of their own.

  • 1932 - November 16

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    The custodians of the Monmouth County Library system meet in Red Bank Library and form the “Monmouth County Library Round Table”. Henry S. White, President of Red Bank Public Library, is voted to be the Board President, and meetings will occur in the spring and fall. The next meeting will be in May at the home of Mrs. B. D. Yard in Allentown.

    At the first meeting, every participant reported increased circulation numbers. This is partly due to the number of people who are now unemployed and are taking advantage of improving their leisure time.

    The largest problem among the libraries now is the lack of new books due dwindling funds. The library’s funding, which usually comes from taxes, is now unavailable due to so many being unemployed and living in financial strain. Round Table participants are asked to let the public know of the situation.

  • 1933 - January 19

    The Neptune Library Branch has gained 140 new registrations, and doubled its books in circulation from the year before.

    The library is located at 204 Ridge Avenue, and is open on Thursdays 2-5PM. In 1931, more than 8,500 books circulated. Now, more than 16,000 books circulated in the prior year.

  • 1933 - May 26

    The Monmouth County Library Commission entertains the organization’s library custodians at the home of Mrs. B. D. Yard – a member of the County Commission.

    50 people attended and met with the other County Commissioners while enjoying various events and lectures. Custodians came from 27 towns and boroughs.

  • 1935 - January 2

    The Neptune Branch now says that 6,707 books circulated in the past year, which was a significant drop. The branch says that this is because it could not get more new books, which has impacted patron demand.

    Officials say the fire resulted from spontaneous combustion of debris in a different compartment of the garage.

    It is reported that 2,099 books were loaned to schoolchildren in Wanamassa during the 1935-36 school year. But this past school year increased its circulation requests to 3,122, showing an increase in interest in books at schools.

  • 1940 - April 18

    The Monmouth County Library Commission’s annual report shows that 1,049 new borrowers joined the system, and 360,518 titles circulated among 67 library stations, 57 schools, and 49 trailer stops.

  • 1941 - March 6

  • 1941 - May 18

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County’s newer book trailer celebrates its 3rd birthday.

    The trailer’s services are described as being as reliable as the USPS, since it operates in all weather conditions except when so much snow falls that excavation is impossible.

    Its circulation numbers doubled between 1939 and 1940 - entirely through word-of-mouth recommendations, and it still brings materials to out-of-reach communities – such as Vanderburg, Holmeson, Oak Glen, and El. The trailer travels along 10 routes on a bi-weekly schedule.

    Inside, there is a a desk, chairs, racks, and lights. Patrons can choose among 800 titles, and fans are installed in the summertime, then a small coal stove heats the trailer’s inside during the winter.

    Items from the book trailer are fine-free when overdue, since borrowers could easily miss when the book trailer visits again, and the aim of the county is to encourage use of its facilities within reason rather than discourage the borrowing of books.

    It is still the only mobile trailer library in the state, and there are, “probably very few in the United States.”

  • 1943 - February 25

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    The library commission announces that wartime conditions have forced the book trailer to temporarily cease its delivery services.

    Instead, the station wagon will be used as a “book truck” since it uses less gas – which is now being rationed. Also, the station wagon can be easily driven by other people, which is valuable as drivers are drafted into the war.

    The changes to delivery services will go into effect on March 1st.

  • 1943 - March 5

    Oceanport announces that a library station will open at the firehouse - to remedy for losing its ability to get services from the book trailer. The new branch will be open every other Tuesday, from 1 p.m. to 3 p.m.

  • 1945 - April 5

    Monmouth County Library reports that 43,595 books circulated from 152 locations across 13 townships and 24 boroughs in the past year.

    The library also received more than 5,000 specific book requests by residents, and the book truck has delivered 8,000 more books than the year before.

  • 1946 - November 17

    With wartime restrictions gone, Monmouth County Library announces that its demands and services are increasing.

    The county system now serves 58 branch libraries and 61 schools.

    Two book mobile are also in service again, and the second book mobile can hold 1,500 titles.

  • 1947 - June 1

    The county library creates a public contest, and asks people to submit their best ideas for a sign that will be displayed at library stations.

    The submissions must be on a paper or cardboard that measures 12x18 inches, and should include a symbol that is simple, and which can be recognized easily from 30 feet away. The symbol should pertains to books, and the design may not use more than 2 colors - a background color and a content color.

    Entries will be submitted to the Asbury Park Press, and the winner will be announced on June 22. The winner will receive a $50 prize, and a copy of the winning sign will be immediately flown at the National Library Convention in California. After the convention, the sign will be used at all libraries and book stations in the county system.

  • 1947 - June 29

  • 1949 - April

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    The book trailer is still unique in the state, and is now celebrating its eleventh year of service.

    Book deliveries are funded by the communities it serves, and it drives 300 miles each week. It is staffed with a librarian trained in Reader’s Advisory and a children’s specialist to work with schools. Librarians working in in the book trailer also report that one person or home will often accept responsibility for taking out books for entire neighborhoods during book trailer visits.

    Book stations are still visitied by a book trailer every 6 weeks to have their collections updated, and there are no limitations on how many items a person may check out.

    Books are rarely returned late, and there is a fine now for late items: 1 cent per day for each item that is late.

  • 1949 - December 2

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Areas that joined the Monmouth County Library system now pay a library tax that comes out to 3/10ths of a mill on the assessed valuation of a property.

    A property assessed at $1,500 - which was the average valuation of a property in Monmouth County at this time, would lead to a tax of 45 cents.

    The commission now seeks to raise the library tax to 4/10ths of a mill, to expand and improve the library’s services. This would mean that the same property owner would now pay 60 cents per year.

    There has been a 16% increase in the area’s population since 1940, and the additional funds would enable evening hours in rural areas, and a new and more reliable “bookmobile” can be purchased for $6,800. Currently, the bookmobile makes weekly deliveries to 64 municipalities, and 60 schools, as well as 45 trailer stops. It also regularly visits the Marlboro state hospital, the county welfare house in Freehold, and Fitkin hospital.

  • 1950 - February 5

    Neptune opens a new library branch in the basement of the West Grove Church on Atkins Avenue. It is open on Tuesdays from 2-5PM.

    The branch hopes to breathe a new love for books and reading into children – who are now commonly seen watching TV and movies rather than holding a book.

  • 1950 - June 9

    Residents in Allentown and its surrounding communities come out to inspect Monmouth County Library's new Bookmobile, which will be used to bring library services to rural residents.

    The Bookmobile has an all-metal body, shelves for 3,000 books, and desk space for a team of librarians and clerks to work on. It includes the latest safety features and lighting devices, as well as 2 skylights.

  • 1950 - July 5

    At 3:30PM, in front of the Asbury Park Press office at Press Plaza, the State Library Director - Roger McDonough - stresses the need for truth and knowledge to maintain democracy while dedicating Monmouth County Library’s Bookmobile.

    From 10AM to 3:30PM, the older vehicle was also parked nearby, for the public to compare both vehicles.

    The new Bookmobile is 16 feet long, spacious, and can hold 1,800 books in total - thanks to additional cab space over the engine, which can house 1,000 additional books.

    At night, the interior uses fluorescent lights, and some racks have been made to accommodate for oversized books and children’s books. It also has the newest heating and venting capabilities.

    The Bookmobile will make deliveries 5 days a week as it visits 59 library stations and 42 trailer stops. The older book trailer will be devoted exclusively to educational services for the 60 county schools that have partnered with the county library system.

  • 1950 - August 9

  • 1955 - August 8

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    While many say libraries aren’t worth the investment with cars and TVs today, Miss Julia H. Killian – MCL Director – says reading interest increases. Whenever a book is reviewed on TV, people want the book and the library is swamped with requests for it.

    All libraries agree they need more money. State Library Association will recommend in the fall that $2.50 per person a year should be spent on books – more than twice of what Ocean County gives, and three times more than what MCL donates to its library.

    An increase in tax going to the libraries will need to be where this comes from, or it will have to come from the state.

    The money would be needed for all central headquarters operations and more field workers.

    The State Library Development Committee has recommended that state aid be used. State money should be used for integrating, but not controlling local library purchases and cataloging.

    The report says county librarians also ought to have working agreements with all city libraries, and city libraries ought to coordinate in several ways:

    Have central buildings for combining collections. Pooling orders for book to avoid duplication. Operating one catalog department. Sharing specialized personnel. Coordinating public, school, and college libraries.

    The Library Extension Committee feels that the library should be seen like a lifetime school curriculum. There should be mutual understanding and cooperation between libraries and schools.

    The following concepts should be emphasized:

    Helping children achieve developmental tasks appropriate for each age level and provide for the wide range of different students in a given class. Using text books for reference and the library for supplemental information.

    This can be accomplished by providing a library coordinator who helps libraries work these out themselves, but the ultimate problem is to get school libraries to provide reference and text books and the public library to give supplementary cultural reading without unnecessary duplication. It would be costly, but worthwhile.

  • 1955 - August 9

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    What’s wrong with the libraries? First, funds. The structure of the state system is weak and confusing, and the independence of local libraries is a cause and result of this. For example, Beach Haven decided it will no longer service the rest of Long Beach Island – so the county will have to do it. So another library will be needed, or more bookmobile services. Another instance of duplicating books and using more money to produce the same amount of service or – what really happens – having less service provided.

    There aren’t enough qualified librarians and not enough incentive (money) for students to study librarianship.

    There’s too many small libraries and they can’t get the needed discounts, they duplicate catalogs of books, they can't make full use of the money they have, and can’t provide the minimum number of books needed. In summary: as individual units, they can’t have the money and resources to build an adequate library.

    Children do better, since they have school libraries.

    Another weakness is the lack of cooperation between schools and libraries. College libraries have great reference material that could be used if an agreement existed between Universities and Public Libraries. Public libraries aren’t able to select books with an eye toward providing supplementary reading to school libraries. There is rarely anyone with authority to provide a basic policy for selecting books for all libraries in an area.

    The State Library Development Committee said every individual should be entitled to the following:

    A library or bookmobile stop near their home or near a shopping center, supervised by a professional librarian A central library, 30 minutes from a home, with 25,000 well-selected, up-to-date titles organized in a working collection – along with magazines, films, recordings, an extensive collection of reference sources, a comfortable reading room, and a staff that includes a children’s specialist. A program of activities and special services for adults and children – which is non-existent even at the county level. A plan where resources from research libraries and state universities can be made public.

    One other problem that has no final solution or report: the selection of books.

    Librarian select at the county by reading book reviews, answering public demand, and meeting Library Association standards. But there’s much room for error, the budget is limited, and libraries never have enough best sellers to meet the demand.

    For example, two authors may be highly popular, but one author happens to be on the best sellers list so it is ordered by all libraries, while the second author is left out.

    The question is whether libraries should reflect the level of American culture or raise the level. Unfortunately, budgets will probably determine this answer.

  • 1955 - August 10

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County residents pay half a cent per year for every $1,000 assessed on someone’s property. This is $156,000 each year, to pay for facility maintenance, bookmobiles, sublibraries, salaries, and $18,000 goes to books. The ultimate way you judge a library is by tis books, and in Monmouth County each person pays 6 cents a year for books – the third lowest rate in the state.

    MCL receives 70 cents per person, which is the 13th highest in the state. The State Library Association will recommend 2.50 per person, as a minimum for adequate services. Out of the 70 cents given, less than half goes to paying salaries – making the county 16th in the state.

    Meanwhile, Ocean County gets $62,000 each year, an average of $1.60 per person. 23.5 cents goes for books. Only Sussex County has a higher rate: 24 cents, and 60% goes to librarian salaries.

    Ocean county residents pay nearly 4x more for books and 2x more for salaries compared to Monmouth county.

    Librarians learned that $1.50 is the minimum needed for a run-of-the-mill library. 2 million people in the 13 counties get lower than $1 per capita service – including Monmouth county residents.

    Monmouth circulates 3 books each year for every 2 residents. Ocean circulates more than 5 books for every 2 people. Figuring costs by circulations show that Monmouth residents pay 50 cents each book, while Ocean County residents pay 40 cents each.

    Ocean county’s 2 bookmobiles circulate 32,000 books each year, and 25,000 of them circulate in the summer – when bookmobiles make special runs to the shore. Total circulation in 1954 was 87,000.

    In Monmouth County Library, the bookmobiles delivered 57,000 books each year and the overall circulation in 1954 was 132,000.

    These numbers don’t include how many times people read, because the books also often go to places such as public schools or other public libraries, where they may be read 15 times or more.

    MCL does more business from mail deliveries – 9,000 compared with 376 in Ocean County. Ocean county says it answered 22,000 reference questions, and MCL says 4,000 registrants exist, which is 4x more than ocean.

  • 1956 - September 22

    The writer explains that being open past 4:30 p.m. would be more practical and convenient for commuters, businesses, and to working professionals, and that enabling the library to be more available to these communities would make a better community overall.

  • 1956 - September 30

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    One of the biggest challenges for libraries across the state now is staff shortages.

    According to findings from the Commission to Study Library Services in New Jersey, 419 of the state's 567 municipalities offered low compensation to recent graduates in the field. The average salary at this time is $2,500 per year, with little chance for raises in the first few years of working.

    Furthermore, the budgets given to libraries now are often too small to do more than a selective refreshment of certain parts of a collection, and librarians are often inheriting older buildings that are too small to maintain the books and equipment that should be included in a modern library.

  • 1956 - November 1

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County Library now delivers books to 5 schools twice a semester, to service 30,000 students and their teachers.

    The American Library Association at this time says that adequate services by a county library should give 5 books to every child that is attending school. Accomplishing this would require each person to pay $1.50. However, Monmouth County residents pay less than $1 per family.

    After tax money is used to pay for salaries, book purchases, maintenance, and upkeep for 2 bookmobiles that visit more than 80 rural stations and branch libraries in municipalities that have little or no local library services, the resulting $66,700 for books does not provide more than 1.5 books per child. As a result, only a total of 25 can be checked out by a given class when in 3rd grade or less.

  • 1957 - February 7

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Rowland C. Mauch begins a one-person campaign to make a new library building for the Neptune Branch.

    The Neptune library is currently in the Municipal Building on Main Street, and has been part of the Monmouth County Library system since 1931. Mauch now believes it is time for the growing township to have a full library.

    The county library currently change's Neptune's 300-book collection every 3 months. However, if another branch requests one of the books, then Mauch must wrap the book up and mail it to Freehold. Because of this, the most popular books do not at the Neptune Branch very long.

    Mauch is on a speaking campaign to win interest in moving the library, and to find book donations. She has spoken to Parent-Teacher Associations, and is now in service clubs and other civic groups to find support. Mauch also wants to establish memorials within books to earn financial donations, and tells the Asbury Park Press that residents must want a library, and do their part to get it.

    The Township Committee states that they are waiting to see how the current library grows and to see the final demand for books before they give Mauch their support. The Committee does not want to go overboard, but says they will do what they can to give more financial support for what is most needed.

  • 1957 - May 5

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County Library Director, Julia Killian, says the cost of books has risen 75% in the past 25 years.

    Monmouth County Library is the largest library purchasing books in the county, however there still isn't enough funding to comfortably keep the collections modernized while paying for the library to functions - salaries, buildings, maintenance, the book mobiles, supplied, repairs, and more.

    Asbury Park has a permanent population of 18,000 and a summer population of 100,000. Shore towns like this are not only a popular summer resort, but also derive a good deal of income from summer visitors. However, local library cannot meet the needs of its permanent residents, much less the needs of summer population.

    While library staffs work from 9am to 9pm to make the library an “open door” for the community, availability cannot make up for the lack of a library's basic commodity: books.

  • 1957 - May 8

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    An emergency appropriation is approved by the Shrewsbury Borough Council to provide for a public library. The borough has been serviced by the bookmobile for years, and has needed a library for some times.

    The borough will rent a room in the Frank Wildey building on Broad Street to house the library, and the rent will cost $500 for the remainder of this year. $1,800 to $2,000 will also be needed to get the library started.

    The library will have 3,000 books to circulate, will be open 3 days a week, and $500 of new books will be purchased.

  • 1957 - December 1

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Monmouth County Library now has 125,000 books, and service partnerships with 5 elementary schools and 5 high schools. Individual branches in the county system are operated according to each branch's directors.

    Residents and property owners hgain library cards for free, and others can join into the system by paying a deposit that is returned to them when the paying member withdraws. Many summer residents take advantage of this deposit system, and those under 21 must get permission from a parent to join by deposit.

    For convenience, the Branch Manager Librarian at Allenhurst lets nearby communities that are part of the Monm outh County Library system - Loch Arbour, Wanamassa, Interlaken, and the southern part of Deal – use the Allenhurst facilities. The Library Director, Killian, states that they would like this type of inter-community borrowing to extend throughout the county system further: “I’d like to see people get a card good throughout the county”.

    The only county municipalities now that are not part of the system are Asbury Park, Avon, Bradley Beach, Freehold Borough, Keyport, Long Branch, Matawan, Matawan Township, Middletown Township, Red Bank, and Spring Lake. Most of these exempt communities have their own local library facilities instead.

    To be part of the Monmouth County Library system, a municipality must pass a resolution to join in, and the community will need to pay a library tax set by the Board of Freeholders - which the count library answers to. The library tax is based on the valuation of real property.

  • 1957 - December 26

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    Mrs. Mauch is commended for her efforts to speak with clubs, groups, and individuals to find more books and support for the Neptune Library. With cooperation with the American Legion Auxiliary and the Lions’ Club of Neptune, Mauch gained a permanent collection for the Neptune Branch, 500 of which are now available to the public.

    Two years prior, there were no permanent books on the shelves and no active borrowers. Now, there are 200 regular patrons who have made more than 2,700 circulations.

  • 1958 - June 18

    In the summer, the county library places an ad in the Asbury Park Press, announcing to summer visitors that they can use the library for a $1 subscription fee and $5 deposit.

  • 1958 - July 11

    The Holmdel Township Committee announces that they will allow temporary use of the township hall to house a book collection provided by the Monmouth County Library. The collection will rely on volunteer help for circulations.

    The Township Committee also agrees to consider a permanent library.

    Currently, there are no library facilities in Howell except for monthly visits by the county library's bookmobile.

  • 1959 - March 22

    SCROLL DOWN

    SCROLL DOWN

    26 public libraries now exist along the shore – 19 library in Monmouth County, and 7 in Ocean County. In both counties, library buildings exist mostly in larger municipalities, and bookmobile services service the more rural parts.

    One of Monmouth County's Bookmobiles serves about 70 schools, and the other Bookmobile is still devoted to visiting rural areas.

    Fair Haven, Ocean Towship, and Rumson are announcing that they may build new libraries, while Long Branch, Little Silver, Middletown Township, and Raritan Township are planning on making additions to their present library buildings.

    Director Killian also says that changing populations at the Shore has increased demands for better library facilities.

    The Director also says that the headquarters branch housed in the house on Broad Street in Freehold is becoming cramped again.
